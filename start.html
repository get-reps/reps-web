Why You Forget Everything You Read (And How to Fix It)

The forgetting curve is real. Here's the research behind spaced repetition — and why 3 minutes a day beats 3 hours a week.

By the REPS Team 2 min read

Think about the last article you read that genuinely excited you. Can you recall its three main points right now, without looking? If you're hesitating, you're not forgetful — you're experiencing one of the most well-documented findings in cognitive science. It has a name, a shape, and a surprisingly elegant fix.

Ebbinghaus and the Forgetting Curve

In 1885, German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus published Über das Gedächtnis — the first rigorous scientific study of memory. Using himself as the sole subject, he memorised thousands of meaningless syllable strings, then measured precisely how fast he forgot them. The result was the forgetting curve: a graph showing that memory doesn't fade gently. It collapses.

Within 24 hours, we forget roughly 50–70% of new information. Within a week, close to 90% is gone. The drop is steepest in the first hour after learning — a fact that makes passive reading, without any follow-up, almost comically inefficient. Ebbinghaus also found the antidote: each time you successfully retrieve a memory just before it disappears, the curve resets at a higher baseline and decays more slowly next time.

"Without reinforcement, people forget up to 50–70% of new information within 24 hours, and close to 90% within a week."

— Based on Ebbinghaus, Über das Gedächtnis (1885); see also Cepeda et al., Psychological Bulletin (2006)

SM-2: When the Algorithm Does the Remembering for You

The optimal review schedule — revisiting material at expanding intervals, right at the moment it's about to slip — is called spaced repetition. The theory had been around for decades, but it took a Polish researcher named Piotr Woźniak to turn it into something a computer could run.

In 1987, Woźniak developed the SM-2 algorithm for his SuperMemo software. SM-2 assigns every piece of information an easiness factor — a number that adjusts based on how confidently you recalled it. Answer easily and the next review is pushed further out, perhaps weeks. Struggle, and the interval contracts. Each card gets its own personalised forgetting curve, and the algorithm schedules reviews at the precise moment memory needs reinforcing — not before (wasteful), not after (too late).

SM-2, or a close descendant of it, now powers most major spaced repetition software in the world, from Anki to Duolingo. The underlying maths remain largely unchanged because they turned out to be remarkably accurate from the start.

Interleaving: Why Mixing Questions Beats Reviewing One Topic at a Time

Spaced repetition handles when to review. But the research also has strong things to say about how you should sequence questions — and the answer surprises most people.

Most study sessions are blocked: finish everything on topic A, then move to topic B. It feels organised and productive. But a series of studies by Doug Rohrer and colleagues at the University of South Florida found the opposite approach — interleaving questions from different topics within the same session — produces dramatically better results on delayed tests.

"Students who used interleaved practice scored 72% on a test two weeks later. Those who used blocked practice scored 38%."

— Rohrer, Dedrick & Burgess, Psychonomic Bulletin & Review (2014)

Why? Blocked practice lets you answer on autopilot — you already know the next question will be about the same thing. Interleaving forces your brain to identify which concept applies, not just execute it. That act of discrimination is itself a form of retrieval, and it makes memories more durable and more transferable. Interleaving feels harder in the moment. That difficulty is exactly what makes it work.
